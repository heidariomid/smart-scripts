The Premise

The plan: cross an entire country by rail before lunch — almost.

The goal was simple and audacious — board in central Tokyo and ride all the way to Kagoshima, the southern tip of mainland Japan, in a single day. The Japanese rail system is billed as the most advanced in the world and famously never late. This was the test, and a first-ever Shinkansen ride at that.

🚄 What "Shinkansen" means

  • Shinkansen (新幹線) = "new trunk line" — Japan's high-speed bullet train network.
  • Reputation: the most advanced rail system on Earth, and "never delayed."
  • This trip put both claims to a 7-hour, two-train stress test.

🎫 The ticket

  • Class booked: Green Car — Japan's equivalent of business class.
  • Price: ~£200 / $250 for the entire Tokyo→Kagoshima route.
  • Format: scannable QR code at the gates; one-way only.

😅 The rocky start

  • Got lost finding Tokyo Station — couldn't read the signs.
  • Wrong station first (Ueno area); a 20–30 min walk away.
  • Made the train with minutes to spare — "sweating buckets."

The Money Question

Green Car vs Economy — is business class worth it?

He paid for the Green Car (business). After walking all 16 then 8 carriages and comparing, his repeated, unambiguous verdict surprised even him.

🟢 Green Car (Business)

  • 2 + 2 seating — more room, footrest, deep recline.
  • Mobile food/drink service to the seat.
  • Located in the calm middle of the train.
  • "These seats are comfy… you can have a sleep here."

🔵 Economy ("S Work")

  • 3 + 2 seating — slightly tighter, but still excellent.
  • No seat-side ordering, no extras.
  • "If that's economy, that's not much difference to the Green Car."
  • Same smooth, immaculate ride.

If you're booking a bullet train in Japan, just go for the cheapest seat possible — between any of the classes, it's not worth paying more. All the seats are just as good.

— The verdict, repeated three times across the trip

Small Details, Big Picture

Easy-to-miss observations

The kind of details that reveal a system engineered to its bones.

➡️ Every seat faces forward

  • On the entire 16-carriage train, not one seat faced backward — the whole cabin is turned to face the direction of travel.

🗺️ A map on the wall

  • Each carriage carries a printed diagram of the train showing all 16 cars and which are Green — easy orientation when your seat is six cars away.

🌸 Cherry blossom timing

  • The trip coincided with full bloom — "the cherry blossoms in Japan are out, how amazing do they look?" — even glimpsed while sprinting to the station.

🏔️ The missed Mount Fuji

  • The London couple booked the Fuji-facing side of the train on purpose — but cloud cover hid it. A reminder that the view is weather's gamble.

🤢 Too fast to watch

  • Looking out the side window at full speed "makes you feel ill" — the scenery whips past faster than the eye can track.

🍜 Nagoya's miso katsu

  • A passenger insisted Nagoya — otherwise dismissed as a "nothing" middle city — is worth stopping for its miso-katsu "soul food."
The Verdict

Highly efficient, immaculate, comfortable — and cheaper than flying.

Across 1,400 km and 7 hours, the Japanese bullet train delivered on every promise: never a second late, spotless from polished floor to heated toilet seat, and full of remarkably kind people. The only knock — there's "not too much to do" on board; no cafe car, just your seat and the country streaming by. The takeaway: book the cheapest seat, leave plenty of time to find the platform, and let the train do what planes can't here — get you anywhere in Japan, on time, for the price of a budget flight.
